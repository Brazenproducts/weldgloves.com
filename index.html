Overview

Welding gloves are the most process-specific PPE in a welder's kit. The same glove that works perfectly for MIG welding will make you significantly less effective at TIG work — and vice versa. This is because the welding processes generate different levels of radiant heat, require different levels of finger dexterity, and expose gloves to different levels of spatter. MIG and flux-core welding generates heavy spatter that requires thick insulation. TIG welding requires fine finger control to manipulate the torch and filler rod simultaneously — thick gloves make this nearly impossible. Stick arc welding generates the most intense heat and requires the highest protection levels. Understanding which process you primarily do drives glove selection more than any other single factor.

Buying Guide — What to Know Before You Buy

Match Glove to Process

This cannot be overstated: MIG/stick gloves and TIG gloves are fundamentally different products. Using MIG gloves for TIG work results in poor quality welds and fatigue. Using TIG gloves for MIG work is a burn hazard.

Leather Type Matters

Cowhide (grain or split): best spatter resistance, good durability, less dexterity. Deerskin: best dexterity, good heat protection, less spatter resistance. Goatskin: middle ground, good for light MIG. Pigskin: breathable, good durability.

Proper Glove Sizing

Welding gloves that are too large allow the glove to shift during work, reducing protection and control. Too small and circulation is restricted, increasing heat discomfort. Measure your hand circumference at the widest point.

Glove Maintenance

Dry at room temperature after wet work — don't use heat. Apply leather conditioner every 20–30 hours of use to prevent cracking. Replace immediately when leather shows holes, cracks, or thin spots.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need different gloves for MIG and TIG welding?

Yes. MIG gloves are thick and prioritize spatter and heat protection at the cost of dexterity. TIG gloves are thin and prioritize dexterity for torch manipulation. Using MIG gloves for TIG work significantly degrades weld quality.

What's the best leather for welding gloves?

For MIG: split cowhide (spatter-resistant, durable). For TIG: deerskin (thin, dexterous, soft). For stick arc: heavy grain cowhide or Kevlar-reinforced (maximum insulation). No single leather is best for all processes.

How long should welding gloves last?

MIG gloves in professional use: 6–12 months. TIG gloves: 3–6 months (thinner material wears faster). Stick arc gloves: 4–8 months. Gloves used only occasionally can last 3–5 years.

Can I use welding gloves for plasma cutting?

TIG-weight gloves work for plasma cutting since the spatter pattern is different from welding. MIG gloves provide more protection but reduce the dexterity needed for precise cuts. Use the thinnest glove that provides adequate protection for your cut conditions.

What size welding gloves should I order?

Measure your dominant hand circumference at the knuckles (without thumb). Use the manufacturer's size chart — welding glove sizing varies by brand. Most men need L or XL; most women need M.

Are heat-resistant gloves the same as welding gloves?

No. Heat-resistant oven or BBQ gloves lack the spatter and UV radiation resistance of welding gloves. Using non-welding gloves while welding is a safety hazard — the gloves can ignite from UV radiation or melt from spatter.

What makes Tillman gloves worth the premium?

Tillman has been manufacturing welding gloves since 1928. Their sizing is consistent, leather selection is precise for each application, and they inspect at higher quality thresholds than most competitors. Professional welders consistently rate them at the top of the category.

Should gloves be stored in a specific way?

Store flat or hanging — never folded or crumpled, which creates permanent creases that crack under heat. Keep dry and away from oil or solvent contamination, which degrades leather integrity.
